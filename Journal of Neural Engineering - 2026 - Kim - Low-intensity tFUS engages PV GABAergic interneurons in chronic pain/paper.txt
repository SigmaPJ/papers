1. Introduction

2

Treating chronic pain by means of non-pharmacological approaches is urgently needed due

3

to the severe side effects of drugs including abuse, overdose, and addiction, all of which contribute

4

to a significant health pandemic [1–3]. To reduce reliance on pharmacological treatments,

5

neuromodulation strategies targeting pain-processing circuits have been developed [4–9]. Deep

6

brain stimulation (DBS) is effective for movement disorders and has shown promise in managing

7

chronic pain [4,5], but it requires invasive surgical implantation [4,6,8]. Non-invasive

8

neuromodulatory alternatives such as transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) and

9

transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) demonstrated diverse treatment efficacies [10–13];

10

however, they typically influence broad areas and have limited ability to reach deeper brain

11

structures [6,7].

12

Transcranial focused ultrasound (tFUS) is a promising neuromodulation technology with

13

high spatial specificity and penetration depth compared to other non-surgical brain stimulation

14

methods [14–17], enabling non-invasive modulation of targeted brain regions [14–19] along with

15

behavior control [20,21] and treatment of neuropsychological disorders [22–26]. Low-intensity

16

tFUS has recently shown potential for pain treatment in both pre-clinical and human studies. In

17

humans, tFUS targeting the anterior cingulate cortex modulated local activity [27] and alleviated

18

both chronic pain symptoms and responses to acute heat stimuli [28]. Additional evidence

19

demonstrated antinociceptive effects from anterior thalamus stimulation [29] and mood

20

improvements with posterior frontal cortex tFUS in pain patients [30]. In animal models,

21

stimulation of the periaqueductal gray (PAG) has been shown to attenuate formalin-induced

22

nociceptive activity in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord [31], while stimulation of the primary

23

somatosensory cortex (S1), insula, or both regions simultaneously significantly suppressed pain-

4

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.08.680737; this version posted October 8, 2025. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

related behaviors in a humanized sickle cell disease (SCD) mouse model [32,33]. These studies

2

suggest that targeted neuromodulation in the brain can ameliorate pain; however, further

3

investigation is needed to elucidate the underlying mechanisms driving the modulation of pain-

4

related behaviors and neural circuits. A deeper understanding of both cellular and

5

electrophysiological effects, including sustained modulation of neuronal expression and network-

6

level dynamics, will be essential to bridge critical knowledge gaps.

7

In this study, we investigated the mechanisms and safety of low-intensity tFUS via

8

integrated electrophysiological and cellular approaches in a humanized mouse model of chronic

9

pain. We hypothesize that tFUS with a lower pulse repetition frequency (PRF) can significantly

10

activate specific GABAergic interneurons, such as the Parvalbumin (PV) cells, and enhance

11

inhibitory effects in the mouse model of SCD which expresses >99% human sickle hemoglobin

12

and exhibits both severe chronic pain and hyperalgesia [34,35]. We first examined local evoked

13

responses, topographical brain activity, global network dynamics in excitation/inhibition (E/I)

14

balance using flexible 30-channel electroencephalography (EEG) recordings paired with a highly-

15

focused 128-element random array transducer. At the cellular level, we next incorporated

16

immunohistochemistry (IHC) targeting inhibitory circuit markers, including PV and Glutamate

17

Decarboxylase 67 (GAD67), a key GABA-synthesizing enzyme [36-39]. Additionally, we

18

evaluated the safety profile of 14-day repeated tFUS through blinded histological analysis of the

19

same brain samples examined by IHC. This comprehensive approach aimed to uncover how low-

20

intensity tFUS non-invasively modulates the inhibitory circuits at both the cellular and systems

21

levels, thereby providing foundational insights into its translational potential for chronic pain.

20

22

between the array transducer and the hydrophone to closely mimic the in vivo tFUS configuration.

2.3 tFUS stimulation targeting procedures and parameters

2

Mice were anesthetized using 3% isoflurane with a 1 L/min oxygen flow for induction, and

3

1-1.5% isoflurane with a 0.3-0.5 L/min oxygen flow for maintenance. A heating pad was used to

4

5

6

To estimate the targeted brain circuits using a 128-element random array transducer, we

7

followed previously established targeting procedures [32]. Briefly, the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas

8

was referenced to determine the coordinate of the ultrasound focus. The transducer was positioned

9

by visually aligning the transducer’s X-axis with the posterior edge of zygomatic structure and the

10

Y-axis with the mouse’s rostral caudal midline (Fig. 1A-B). We applied single- or multi-session

11

tFUS with specific parameters including tone-burst duration (TBD), ultrasound duration (UD),

12

inter-sonication interval (ISoI), pulse repetition frequency (PRF), and total sonication time (Fig.

13

1C).

3. RESULTS

20

3.1 Single-session tFUS suppressed local theta oscillation consistent with PV-driven

21

inhibition

22

To investigate how specific tFUS parameters modulate intrinsic brain activity in pain

23

circuits, we integrated 30-channel scalp EEG recordings with a 128-element random array

9

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.08.680737; this version posted October 8, 2025. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

ultrasound transducer to evaluate tFUS-evoked brain responses in SCD mice. We first tested the

2

hypothesis that a single session tFUS could perturb local brain activity when precisely targeted to

3

specific pain-processing brain regions. During EEG recordings, following a baseline period,

4

ultrasound pulses were delivered to the right hemisphere’s S1HL region (200-µs TBD, 40 Hz or 3

5

kHz PRF, 100-ms UD, 2-sec ISoI, 20-min total sonication time; Fig. 1C). Using the excitation

6

parameter with 3 kHz PRF, we found significant activation in the targeted S1HL compared with

7

both the negative control and the 40 Hz PRF condition related to the inhibition parameter (Fig.

8

1D).

9

We next investigated whether intrinsic brain rhythms associated with chronic pain could

10

be modulated by single-session tFUS with a PRF of 40 Hz, previously shown to suppress pain-

11

related behaviors in SCD mice. We observed a significant suppression of low frequency brain

12

oscillations, particularly in the theta and alpha bands during 40 Hz tFUS targeting S1HL region

13

compared to the negative control (Fig. 1E). In contrast, 3 kHz tFUS produced excitatory effects,

14

showing a bidirectional pattern when comparing the low versus high PRF responses relative to the

15

negative control (Supplementary Fig. 1). To further investigate the mechanisms underlying tFUS

16

neuromodulation, we performed optogenetic activation of PV-positive interneurons in rats, which

17

significantly reduced theta, alpha, and beta band power (Fig. 1F), suggesting that PV activation

18

suppresses low-frequency oscillations. These findings align with the previous report showing that

19

silencing PV interneurons increases low-frequency activity [48], indicating that active PV

20

interneurons constrain these rhythms and play a central inhibitory role in shaping cortical dynamics.

21

The spectral outcomes of tFUS and optogenetics generally align yet not fully congruent,

22

suggesting that tFUS may engage broader or complementary inhibitory networks compared to the

23

specificity of optogenetic stimulation. Nevertheless, the shared suppression of the theta band

10

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.08.680737; this version posted October 8, 2025. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

suggests that the fast-spiking PV interneurons are key contributors to tFUS effects. While

2

GABAergic interneurons such as PV and somatostatin-positive (SST) cells both influence theta

3

oscillations, prior studies demonstrated that optogenetic suppression of SST reduces spontaneous

4

low-frequency (<30 Hz) activity, whereas suppression of PV increases it [48,49], underscoring

5

their distinct but complementary roles in modulating cortical oscillations. Thus, the observed

6

suppression of theta power following tFUS likely reflect PV-driven inhibition rather than SST

7

mechanisms.

3.2 Multi-session tFUS enhanced network-level inhibitory activity consistent with PV-

10

mediated inhibition

11

In the local brain circuit, single-session tFUS stimulation with a PRF of 40 Hz resulted in

12

a marked suppression of theta and alpha band power. However, oscillatory changes alone may not

13

fully capture the complex dynamics of brain networks, particularly the balance between excitation

14

and inhibition. We therefore analyzed the aperiodic exponent of EEG, computed by fitting the

15

slope of the signal’s power spectrum as a marker of E/I balance (Fig. 2A). The E/I balance is a

16

crucial aspect of neural circuit function, influencing the network's ability to process information

17

[45]. Shifts in this balance can indicate whether cortical activity becomes more excitatory reflected

18

by a lower aperiodic exponent, or more inhibitory indicated by a higher aperiodic exponent

19

[44,50,51]. We found a significant reduction in the aperiodic exponent in female SCD mice relative

20

to female wild-type (Fig. 2B), reflecting a disrupted E/I balance commonly associated with chronic

21

pain features.

22

We then applied a single-session tFUS in female SCD mice; however, it did not

23

significantly alter E/I balance, suggesting a brief 20-min stimulation may be insufficient to induce

11

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.08.680737; this version posted October 8, 2025. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

an enhancement of global inhibitory activity (Fig. 2C). A 14-day multi-session tFUS (1 h/day) was

2

then implemented, and the change in E/I balance was assessed by resting-state EEG recordings

3

obtained the day after the final stimulation session (Fig. 2D). The multi-session tFUS resulted in

4

a significant increase in the aperiodic exponent in female SCD mice, indicating a shift toward

5

increased inhibitory activity, approaching levels observed in wild-type controls (Fig. 2E and G).

6

Interestingly, an additional 20-min tFUS following the 14-day stimulation further significantly

7

increased the aperiodic exponent compared to single-session tFUS in SCD mice, suggesting an

8

enhanced network-level inhibitory effect potentially driven by synaptic plastic changes (Fig. 2F

9

and I). For comparison, we further found that optogenetic activation of PV interneurons leads to a

10

robust increase in the aperiodic exponent, mirroring the strengthened inhibitory function induced

11

by multi-session tFUS, whereas activating SST interneurons significantly decreases the aperiodic

12

exponent (Supplementary Fig. 2). These findings support the hypothesis that PV-mediated, rather

13

than SST-driven, inhibition underlines the electrophysiological effects of tFUS.

3.3 Multi-session tFUS enhanced local expressions of GAD67 and PV interneurons

16

As repeated tFUS stimulations induced a significant and sustained increase in global

17

inhibitory electrophysiological activity, we investigated cellular changes after multi-session 40 Hz

18

PRF tFUS using IHC analysis and blinded assessment (Fig. 3A). Assessment revealed significantly

19

higher GAD67-positive cells in the stimulated region compared to the contralateral side (Fig. 3B-

20

C), whereas no such difference was observed in the sham-treated group (Supplementary Fig. 3A).

21

These findings suggest that tFUS enhances inhibitory GABAergic signaling by increasing the

22

capacity for GABA synthesis, as reflected by elevated levels of GAD67.

12

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.08.680737; this version posted October 8, 2025. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

Building on the observed upregulation of GAD67, we next addressed the question of

2

whether tFUS could modulate specific subtypes of GABAergic interneurons, particularly PV-

3

expressing neurons, based on our findings related to theta oscillations, distinct E/I balance patterns,

4

and prior evidence involving optogenetic inactivation of PV neurons [48]. Given their important

5

role in maintaining E/I balance and known vulnerability in various neurological conditions [45],

6

we sought to determine whether PV interneurons are upregulated following multi-session tFUS

7

stimulation. Stimulation of left S1HL significantly increased the number of PV-positive cells at

8

the site relative to the contralateral region (Fig. 3D-E), but not from sham treatment

9

(Supplementary Fig. 3B). These findings suggest that tFUS strengthens local inhibitory networks

10

by promoting activity or expression of the PV GABAergic neurons.

11

We further examined whether non-neuronal cell types such as microglia and astrocytes

12

might also contribute. Since GABA is widely expressed in the brain, we alternatively used the H-

13

score, a semi-quantitative method for evaluating protein expression [52,53], to compare GABA

14

immunoreactivity between ipsilateral and contralateral regions. H-score analysis revealed that

15

neither tFUS nor sham treatment led to significant changes in GABA expression within microglia

16

and astrocytes across hemispheres (Fig. 3F-G and Supplementary Fig. 3C-D). Additionally, Iba1

17

levels remained unchanged following tFUS stimulation (Supplementary Fig. 3E-F), indicating a

18

lack of glial activation. These results suggest that tFUS at 40 Hz modulates GABAergic signaling

19

primarily through neuron-specific pathways in the humanized SCD mouse model.

3.4 Multi-dimensional histological analyses confirmed safety of multi-session tFUS

22

To facilitate potential clinical translation, we conducted a thorough safety evaluation by

23

multi-dimensional histological analyses including H&E, TUNEL, and CD31 immunostaining,

13

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.08.680737; this version posted October 8, 2025. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

which are well-established approaches to evaluate tissue morphology [54], cellular integrity [55],

2

and vascular architecture [56], respectively. Following multi-session tFUS stimulation, H&E

3

staining revealed no gross morphological abnormalities, such as significant infiltration of

4

macrophages or neutrophils, inflammation, necrosis, apoptosis, hemorrhage, fibrosis, vacuolation,

5

or brain damage in either hemisphere under tFUS relative to the sham-treated group (Fig. 4A and

6

Supplementary Fig. 4A), reinforcing the structural safety of the protocol. Additionally, TUNEL

7

staining showed no evidence of apoptotic cell death as no TUNEL-positive nuclei were detected

8

in either brain region of tFUS-treated or sham-treated animals (Fig. 4B and Supplementary Fig.

9

4B). Considering that vascular structures in the SCD mouse model may exhibit heightened

10

vulnerability to mechanical perturbations12, we further examined vascular integrity using CD31, a

11

widely accepted marker of endothelial cells. CD31 immunostaining demonstrated similar vascular

12

distribution without signs of pathological angiogenesis, vessel disruption, or hemorrhage in both

13

hemispheres from tFUS-treated and sham-treated groups (Fig. 4C and Supplementary Fig. 4C).

14

Taken together, these results suggest that multi-session tFUS with a PRF of 40 Hz does not induce

15

detectable cytotoxic effects or vascular damage in the brain samples used in the IHC analysis.

DISCUSSION

18

Prior studies have highlighted the potential of tFUS in pain management. However, how

19

tFUS mediates its effects is still poorly understood. In this study, we have demonstrated that low-

20

intensity tFUS with a PRF of 40 Hz engaged PV GABAergic interneurons in a humanized mouse

21

model of chronic pain using combined electrophysiological and molecular approaches. These

22

included enhanced local and global inhibitory activity in the pain-processing brain circuit, as

23

indicated by suppression of low-frequency oscillations at S1HL, elevated aperiodic exponent

14

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.08.680737; this version posted October 8, 2025. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

derived from resting-state EEG, and increased GAD67-immunoreactivity (i.r.) and PV

2

interneuron-i.r. in the stimulated region. Compared to high-intensity tFUS used in ablative

3

protocols, which eliminate cellular activity in the targeted area but risk collateral damage to

4

adjacent healthy circuits potentially leading to adverse side effects [57], this low-intensity tFUS

5

enables targeted neuromodulation while preserving structural and functional integrity. Importantly,

6

we showed tFUS effects without evidence of glial activation, cellular apoptosis, or vascular

7

disruption, confirming safety and neuron-specificity. This specificity underscores its potential as

8

a non-invasive and precise neuromodulation approach for chronic pain management, paving the

9

way for promising therapeutic applications.

10

Our results demonstrated that 40 Hz tFUS led to upregulation of both GAD67 and PV

11

expressions, two molecular hallmarks of the GABAergic inhibitory system. Given prior evidence

12

that GAD67 deficiency in PV interneurons resulted in impaired inhibitory transmission and

13

cortical disinhibition [39,58], the observed post-tFUS upregulation supports the restoration of

14

inhibitory activity. GAD67 is the enzyme that catalyzes synthesis of the inhibitory

15

neurotransmitter GABA from glutamate [36], and is currently being explored as a therapeutic

16

target for treating pain, including via gene therapy approaches [59,60]. Recent study in SCD

17

patients shown elevated brain glutamate, potentially suggesting the correlation between the

18

excitatory neurotransmitter and pain hypersensitivity [61]; thus, increased GAD67 expression may

19

help counterbalance this excitatory drive by enhancing the glutamate to GABA conversion. Our

20

approach utilizing non-invasive tFUS led to upregulation of GAD67, supporting its potential as a

21

convenient and cell-targeted strategy for pain relief. In parallel, GABAergic neurons in S1

22

primarily consist of PV, SST, and vasoactive intestinal peptide (VIP) interneurons [62]. These

23

inhibitory neuron subtypes differ in distribution, firing properties, and synaptic targets [63].

15

Among them, PV interneurons are the dominant GABAergic population, characterized by rapid,

2

high-frequency spiking and strong perisomatic inhibition. Their loss significantly heightens pain

3

sensitivity [64–66], highlighting the relevance of PV GABAergic neurons for effective pain

4

treatment. In our study, we observed a significant increase in PV-positive cell expression in the

5

tFUS-stimulated area, positioning tFUS as a cell-type specific modality in this context.

6

A holistic interpretation of both electrophysiological and IHC findings revealed two

7

complementary clinical applications for the use of tFUS with a PRF of 40 Hz. A single-session

8

tFUS induced suppression of low-frequency EEG rhythms, consistent with short-term circuit

9

inhibition, but did not alter global E/I balance. Thus, it appeared effective for providing rapid relief

10

from acute or tonic pain, which typically arose from inflammation, healing, or mild injury and is

11

generally reversible. It modulated cortical excitability locally and temporarily without inducing

12

detectable morphological changes. As a non-pharmacological alternative, tFUS holds particular

13

promise for patients who may be contraindicated for pharmacological analgesics, which are

14

associated with risks such as gastrointestinal distress, organ toxicity, dependency, and overdose

15

[1–3,67,68]. In contrast, multi-session tFUS accumulated over time, engaging inhibitory circuits

16

through potential synaptic plasticity rather than short-term modulation. These findings aligned

17

with prior results from repetitive TMS, where multiple TMS stimulation induced inhibitory

18

plasticity in cortical networks [69]. This adaptive reorganization of a previously dysregulated

19

inhibitory circuit highlighted a novel therapeutic mechanism of low-PRF tFUS, complementing

20

previously studied pulse-locked neuromodulation or mechanosensitive ion channel effects [70,71].

21

By showing elevated PV-i.r. and GAD67-i.r. alongside persistent electrophysiological changes,

22

our study provided post-stimulation mechanistic evidence of functional network remodeling.

23

While previous reports have focused on acute electrophysiological responses, behavioral outcomes,

16

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.08.680737; this version posted October 8, 2025. The copyright holder for this preprint

1

or self-reported pain ratings [28–33], our findings expanded the mechanistic landscape to include

2

molecular and cellular adaptations in inhibitory circuits driven by repeated subthreshold

3

entrainment of PV neurons. Additionally, when single-session tFUS was applied to 14-day-treated

4

SCD mice, the stimulation effect was significantly greater compared to that observed with single-

5

session tFUS in naïve SCD mice, indicating that non-invasively repetitive stimulations can drive

6

synaptic plasticity even when each session is subtle and transient. Importantly, we observed no

7

signs of cellular or vascular damage across the 14-day stimulation compared to controls, which is

8

an essential safety consideration for any long-term neuromodulation therapy. These results

9

positioned low-intensity tFUS with 40 Hz PRF as a versatile and precision intervention, with

10

flexible dosing paradigms tailored for different clinical needs: single-session use for episodic pain

11

control, and multi-session delivery for chronic pain alleviation through inhibitory circuit

12

restoration. Moreover, the combined use of electrophysiological and immunohistochemical

13

endpoints provided cross-validated evidence for a reliable, non-invasive biomarker with

14

translational relevance. The power spectral changes and the aperiodic exponent derived from EEG,

15

a clinically accessible modality, closely reflected shifts in cortical inhibitory activity and mirrored

16

molecular findings of GAD67 and PV-positive interneurons upregulation. This convergence of

17

readouts suggested that EEG-based measurements may serve as practical surrogate biomarkers for

18

monitoring neuromodulatory engagement in clinical settings.

19

While our study provided multi-level evidence that repeated 40 Hz tFUS strengthened

20

cortical inhibitory activity and promoted long term circuit reorganization, several directions

21

remained to be explored. First, although the use of PV and GAD67 markers was deliberate and

22

mechanistically informative, future studies could incorporate a broader panel of IHC markers to

23

map the GABAergic and Glutamatergic responses more comprehensively. For example, the

17

1

inclusion of SST, VIP, CaMKII, and GABA receptors could allow for a more detailed dissection

2

of cell type and synapse specific changes. Our results aligned with the overall low-frequency

3

power suppression observed during optogenetic activation of PV neurons, the statistical

4

significance inconsistency in the beta band could suggest other potential mechanisms contributing

5

to the inhibition, such as the activation of other GABAergic interneurons or the inhibition of

6

excitatory glutamatergic neurons. Mapping the involvement of additional GABAergic subtypes,

7

including SST, VIP, and their synaptic interactions with excitatory neurons would greatly enhance

8

our understanding of circuit-level reorganization and the precision of tFUS neuromodulation,

9

given the distinct roles these subtypes contribute to the overall sensory perception [72]. Second,

10

when grand-averaging EEG responses across 17 mice, we observed a relatively broader

11

distribution of activity (Supplementary Fig. 5), likely reflecting inter-animal anatomical variability

12

including slight differences in electrode placement, skull thickness, and cortical geometry, which

13

can influence the precise localization of evoked responses. Although this variability is less critical

14

given the relatively thin mouse skull, future studies should consider individualized targeting

15

strategies or improved anatomical registration approaches to better align responses across subjects.

16

Lastly, the translational pathway from rodent models to human application required careful

17

calibration of stimulation parameters including acoustic pressures and stimulation pulse paradigm.

18

In this study, we used humanized SCD mouse model; however, careful consideration must be

19

given to the selection of stimulation parameters due to fundamental biological differences between

20

rodents and humans, including differences in cortical organization and interneuron diversity [73].

21

Computational modeling, paired with validation in large animal models, will be critical to defining

22

safe and effective stimulation protocols for human use.

4
